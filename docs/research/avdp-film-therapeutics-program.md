## The Question

How do media format constraints suppress authentic human expression, and what does recovery look like when the format is designed against the suppression rather than for the platform?

Every media format carries an optimization inside it. Runtime constraints, platform requirements, audience retention curves, production norms, advertiser comfort. At some point these constraints stop serving the subject and start suppressing them. What gets produced is what the format can accommodate, not what the person actually is. The participant edits themselves in advance of the format. What reaches the audience is a format-compliant version of something real.

AVDP (A Very Distant Perspective) is a video podcast series engineered as a counter-format. Each design element — the removal of smartphones, the live-mixed ambient music, the long-form temporal structure, the absence of production pressure — is a hypothesis about the specific mechanism by which conventional formats suppress authentic expression. The counter-format tests those hypotheses by removing the suppressants and measuring how much more of the person comes through.

The **Format Effectiveness Measure (FEM)** is the diagnostic instrument. It measures the signed gap between what a subject expresses in an unconstrained counter-format setting and what a conventional format would have allowed through. A large FEM score means the conventional format was suppressing significantly. The counter-format AVDP produces is the therapeutic environment. The FEM measures whether it works.

This research program also pursues three nested questions about what the counter-format produces: whether authentic disclosure on camera changes how subjects remember and relate to their own experience, whether audiences who witness authentic expression are measurably changed by it, and whether the recordings constitute a corpus of ecological validity rare enough to matter for affective computing.

## Institutional Context

Polarity Lab's thesis is that designed environments have a second threshold. Below it, the environment extends the capacity it was built to serve. Beyond it, optimization for the environment's own metrics begins to suppress the capacity it was built to develop. Media formats crossed this threshold when their optimization for platform performance became optimization against authentic expression.

Each lab at Polarity builds either a diagnostic instrument — a scoring tool that produces a reproducible number for something not currently measured — or a therapeutic environment — a designed experience that produces a specific, measurable change in the people who pass through it.

The AVDP Lab builds both.

The **Format Effectiveness Measure** is the diagnostic instrument. It takes two inputs: what a subject expresses in the AVDP counter-format environment and what that same subject expresses in a conventional interview format. The FEM score is the signed gap between them — a measure of how much authentic expression the conventional format suppressed. Zero means no gap: the conventional format allowed everything through. A large positive score means the counter-format produced materially more authentic expression than the conventional format would have.

**AVDP** is the therapeutic environment. Its design elements are not aesthetic choices. Each is a specific environmental manipulation grounded in the literature on what suppresses and enables authentic self-disclosure. Phone removal, ambient music, long-form pacing, absence of production interruption — each has a documented causal mechanism. Together they constitute a counter-format engineered to maximize the FEM in the direction of authentic expression.

The additional institutional stakes are real. Loneliness is classified as a global health epidemic. Holt-Lunstad et al. (2015) demonstrated that social isolation carries mortality risk equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes per day. Twenge et al. (2019) documented a generational collapse in in-person social interaction among U.S. adolescents. The clinical infrastructure for addressing this does not yet exist. If authentic conversation in a designed counter-format produces measurable prosocial effects in both subjects and audiences — and if the recordings constitute a rare corpus of ecologically valid affective data — AVDP is building part of that infrastructure.

## Foundational Literature

### 1. Przybylski, A. K., & Weinstein, N. (2013). Can you connect with me now? How the presence of mobile communication technology influences face-to-face conversation quality. *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships, 30*(3), 237-246.

In two experiments, the mere presence of a mobile phone on a nearby table, without any use or interaction, reduced perceived empathy, closeness, and trust in dyadic conversation. Effects were most pronounced when the topic was personally meaningful. The proposed mechanism is cognitive: phones prime awareness of the wider social network, effectively crowding out the immediate interlocutor.

**Relevance:** Provides the theoretical and empirical basis for AVDP's no-phones-in-frame design element. This is not a production preference; it is an environmental manipulation with documented effect on conversation depth. It is the first variable in our experimental condition.

---

### 2. Misra, S., Cheng, L., Genevie, J., & Yuan, M. (2016). The iPhone Effect: The quality of in-person social interactions in the presence of mobile devices. *Environment and Behavior, 48*(2), 275-298.

A naturalistic field experiment with 100 dyads in Washington D.C. cafes found that conversations held without mobile devices present were rated significantly higher in quality, and participants reported greater empathic concern and feelings of interconnectedness. The study replicated the Przybylski and Weinstein findings outside the laboratory and introduced the concept of "polyconsciousness" — the divided attentional state produced by ambient digital connectivity.

**Relevance:** This is the naturalistic replication that moves the mere-presence finding from a controlled lab to ecological settings, strengthening the claim that AVDP's phone-exclusion protocol operates on a real and consistently observed phenomenon.

---

### 3. Holt-Lunstad, J., Smith, T. B., Baker, M., Harris, T., & Stephenson, D. (2015). Loneliness and social isolation as risk factors for mortality: A meta-analytic review. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 10*(2), 227-237.

A meta-analysis of 70 studies (N > 3.4 million) found that social isolation, loneliness, and living alone were each associated with significantly elevated mortality risk, with odds ratios of 1.29, 1.26, and 1.32 respectively. The effect size is comparable to established behavioral risk factors including obesity. The results held across gender, follow-up period, and world region.

**Relevance:** Establishes the clinical seriousness of the problem this lab addresses. Any intervention that measurably increases the quality or frequency of authentic social connection is, under this evidence, a health intervention. This paper is the public health scaffolding for AVDP's institutional claim.

---

### 4. Twenge, J. M., Spitzberg, B. H., & Campbell, W. K. (2019). Less in-person social interaction with peers among U.S. adolescents in the 21st century and links to loneliness. *Journal of Social and Personal Relationships, 36*(6), 1892-1913.

Using nationally representative samples of U.S. adolescents (N = 8.2 million, 1976-2017), the study documented a sustained decline in face-to-face peer interaction coinciding with the rise of digital media, with adolescents in 2016 spending approximately one hour less per day in in-person social contact than peers in the late 1980s. Cohort-level loneliness increased sharply after 2011. Adolescents combining high social media use with low in-person interaction reported the greatest loneliness.

**Relevance:** Quantifies the scale of the conversational depth problem. The decline is not a cultural observation; it is a measured behavioral shift with a specific temporal signature. AVDP's format, long-form, uninterrupted, non-digital, is a structural counter to the patterns this data documents.

---

### 5. Kramer, A. D. I., Guillory, J. E., & Hancock, J. T. (2014). Experimental evidence of massive-scale emotional contagion through social networks. *PNAS, 111*(24), 8788-8790.

A large-scale experiment (N = 689,003) demonstrated that emotional states propagate through media exposure without direct interaction or nonverbal cues. When positive content was reduced in users' News Feeds, they produced less positive content; when negative content was reduced, the reverse occurred. Emotional contagion was documented occurring entirely through text-based media exposure.

**Relevance:** Provides empirical evidence for the audience-effects hypothesis at the center of AVDP's second aim. If exposure to emotional content in digital media causally shifts the emotional states and subsequent behavior of viewers at scale, then a carefully designed environment for authentic expression, broadcast as video, has a plausible mechanism for producing prosocial behavioral effects in its audience. The Zay-Mateo case (AVDP viewer seeking out maker's work, cross-border connection initiated) is a single-case instance of this mechanism.

---

### 6. Charon, R. (2001). Narrative medicine: A model for empathy, reflection, profession, and trust. *JAMA, 286*(15), 1897-1902.

Foundational statement of narrative medicine, a clinical practice framework built on the therapeutic power of witnessing and being witnessed in story. Charon argues that effective clinical care requires "narrative competence," the ability to absorb, interpret, and act on the stories of others, and documents how reciprocal narrative practices increase patient well-being and deepen provider-patient trust. Empathy is framed as an active cocreative state, not a passive feeling.

**Relevance:** AVDP does not have patients or clinicians, but it shares the structural logic of narrative medicine: that the act of bearing authentic witness to another person's disclosed experience has measurable effects on both the discloser and the witness. This paper gives the lab a clinical literature lineage for the witnessing mechanism.

---

### 7. Bavelas, J. B., Coates, L., & Johnson, T. (2000). Listeners as co-narrators. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 79*(6), 941-952.

Demonstrates that genuine listener responsiveness, not just silence or passive reception, is a co-constitutive act in the production of narrative disclosure. Listeners who are attentive and responsive elicit deeper, more detailed, and more emotionally rich narration from speakers. The quality of listening materially changes what is said.

**Relevance:** Grounds the interview design in a specific claim: AVDP's format is not just about reducing distractions; it is about engineering an interactional context in which substantive co-narration is possible. The ambient music and slow pacing create the acoustic and temporal conditions for this. The host is not incidental; the listening style is the instrument.

---

### 8. Hartmann, T., Stuke, D., & Daschmann, G. (2008). Positive parasocial relationships with drivers affect suspense in racing sport coverage. *Journal of Media Psychology, 20*(1), 24-34. [Canonical review: Horton & Wohl, 1956; updated: Giles, 2002]

The parasocial relationship literature, originating with Horton and Wohl's 1956 concept of "intimacy at a distance," documents that audiences form one-sided but psychologically substantive relationships with media figures. Contemporary updates show these relationships activate the same neural empathy networks as real social bonds, and that parasocial breakups generate genuine grief responses. Research specifically on podcasts finds that long-form audio self-disclosure by hosts generates the strongest parasocial intimacy, exceeding visual formats for perceived authenticity.

**Relevance:** AVDP's audience effects are not merely hoped for; they are predicted by the parasocial literature. When a viewer watches a genuine, extended, emotionally present conversation, they are not a passive consumer. They are in a functionally social encounter. This literature defines the mechanism by which watching AVDP could change how viewers behave in their own social lives.

### 9. Adler, F. H. B. (2025). Analysing podcast intimacy: Four parameters. *Convergence, 31*(1) [published 2023/2024]. *Convergence.*

Proposes a four-parameter model for analyzing how audio podcasts generate intimacy: aural contact (the quality of voice transmission), physical proximity (microphone placement as simulated closeness), community (shared listener identity), and discourse style (register, spontaneity, self-disclosure). Finds that podcasts that score high across all four parameters generate the strongest listener identification and relational investment.

**Relevance:** AVDP is a video podcast with ambient audio as a structural element. This framework gives the lab a testable, operationalizable model for why the format produces its effects. The lab can score its own episodes on these parameters and correlate scores against engagement and listener-reported connection outcomes.

---

### 10. Calvo, R. A., & D'Mello, S. (2010). Affect detection: An interdisciplinary review of models, methods, and their applications. *IEEE Transactions on Affective Computing, 1*(1), 18-37.

Comprehensive review of the affective computing field through 2010, establishing the central technical challenges: the ground truth problem (self-reported emotion does not reliably align with externally observed signals), the ecological validity problem (lab-elicited emotions do not transfer to real-world settings), and the annotation problem (inter-rater reliability degrades on ambiguous or context-dependent emotional states). Field accuracy in controlled settings at the time ranged from 70-90%; real-world performance was substantially lower.

**Relevance:** This is the first canonical articulation of the precise problems AVDP's corpus is positioned to help solve. The lab's recordings are naturalistic, long-form, multimodal (face, voice, body, temporal context), and produced under conditions designed to elicit genuine rather than performed emotional expression. This is the kind of data the field has needed and lacked.

---

### 11. Schuller, B. W., & Schuller, D. M. (2021). A review on five recent and near-future developments in computational processing of emotion in the human voice. *Emotion Review, 13*(1), 44-50.

Reviews the state of speech-based emotion recognition, noting that controlled-conditions accuracy has plateaued in the 80-90% range while real-world deployment performance remains significantly lower. Identifies the core bottleneck as a shortage of naturalistic training data with reliable ground-truth labeling. Notes that context, topic, and conversational role all substantially affect vocal affect expression in ways current datasets cannot capture.

**Relevance:** Directly names the supply problem that AVDP is positioned to address. Long-form conversational recordings with topic-known context, consistent acoustic environments, and post-session participant annotation represent a dataset architecture no current benchmark approaches.

---

### 12. Ganis, G., & Schendan, H. E. (2022). Through the looking glass: A scoping review of cinema and video therapy. *Frontiers in Psychology, 12*, 732246.

A scoping review of 38 studies on cinema and video-based therapeutic interventions, finding positive effects in 36. Studies documented improvements in behavioral outcomes in autism spectrum disorder and schizophrenia populations, reduced anxiety and improved quality of life in gynecologic cancer patients, and improvements in emotional regulation and social reintegration for veterans coping with PTSD. The review identifies the need for more standardized experimental designs and objective outcome measures as the field's central methodological gap.

**Relevance:** Establishes that video-based therapeutic interventions have a documented evidence base. Critically, the review also maps the gap this lab targets: existing cinema therapy uses pre-produced films as stimuli. No studies in the review examine the therapeutic effects of watching authentic, unscripted conversation between non-professional subjects, which is AVDP's format.

---

### 13. Calvo, R. A., Milne, D. N., Hussain, M. S., & Christensen, H. (2017). Natural language processing in mental health applications using non-clinical texts. *Natural Language Engineering, 23*(5), 649-685.

Demonstrates that non-clinical, naturalistic text and speech produced in everyday digital environments carries recoverable emotional and psychological signal, including markers of depression, anxiety, and social withdrawal, with comparable accuracy to clinical instruments when appropriate computational methods are applied. Argues for expanding affective computing beyond clinical settings into everyday communication corpora.

**Relevance:** Supports the downstream application of AVDP's corpus to mental health research. If non-clinical naturalistic speech already carries clinical signal, then AVDP's recordings, which are specifically designed to elicit depth and authenticity, may carry richer and more granular signal than most available naturalistic data. This positions the corpus as a potential contribution to digital mental health infrastructure.

### 14. Altman, I., & Taylor, D. A. (1973). *Social Penetration: The Development of Interpersonal Relationships.* Holt, Rinehart and Winston.

The foundational theoretical framework for interpersonal self-disclosure, proposing that relationship intimacy develops through progressive layers of disclosure depth and breadth, moving from peripheral to central self-aspects. Disclosure follows a cost-benefit logic and is sensitive to contextual cues of safety, reciprocity, and relevance. The pace of penetration is modulated by environmental and relational signals.

**Relevance:** Social Penetration Theory is the theoretical substrate for AVDP's core format hypothesis. The format is designed to manipulate the environmental inputs that Altman and Taylor identify as governing the pace and depth of disclosure. The ambient music, phone removal, long temporal window, and conversational design are each targeted at the cost-reduction and safety-signaling mechanisms the theory specifies. This gives Aim 1 a testable theoretical architecture.

---

### 15. Nader, K., Schafe, G. E., & Le Doux, J. E. (2000). Fear memories require protein synthesis in the amygdala for reconsolidation after retrieval. *Nature, 406*(6797), 722-726.

Demonstrated that retrieving a consolidated memory returns it to a labile state requiring active reconsolidation to persist. This finding overturned the longstanding assumption that once a memory is stored it remains fixed. Each act of retrieval is not playback but reconstruction: the memory is destabilized, modified by present-state context, and re-stored in altered form. Subsequent work (Schwabe, Nader, & Bhatt, 2012; Schwabe, Nader, Wolf, & Pruessner, 2014) extended the finding to declarative and autobiographical memory in humans, showing that retrieval under novel conditions, including novel emotional or contextual framing, reliably updates the content and affective valence of the re-stored memory.

**Relevance:** AVDP recordings are fixed external artifacts of experiences that exist in parallel as mutable internal memories. When a participant rewatches their own episode, they are retrieving the memory of the original conversation under conditions that differ from the original encoding: they are now an observer, not a participant; the ambient music is a cue but the social pressure is absent; they can pause, rewind, and re-encounter specific moments. Memory reconsolidation theory predicts that this retrieval event will modify the stored memory itself. The question is whether that modification is therapeutically useful, whether rewatching produces a more integrated, less avoidant, or more self-compassionate version of the remembered experience. This is the mechanistic basis for AVDP's memory tool hypothesis.

---

### 16. McAdams, D. P. (2001). The psychology of life stories. *Review of General Psychology, 5*(2), 100-122.

Articulates the narrative identity framework: that human identity is constituted by the internalized, evolving stories people construct about their own lives. These stories are not incidental reflections of identity; they are identity. People selectively remember, interpret, and sequence autobiographical events into coherent narratives that serve psychological functions including continuity, meaning-making, and self-regulation. The stories are revised continuously in response to new experience, and the revision process is itself a site of psychological development.

**Relevance:** AVDP produces a narrative artifact, a recorded conversation in which the participant has disclosed authentically, that becomes a permanent, revisitable element of the participant's life story. Unlike ordinary autobiographical memory, which is reconstructed from internal cues alone, the AVDP recording provides an external, uneditable version of the self at a specific moment. When a participant rewatches, they encounter a past self that cannot be revised by the usual mechanisms of selective memory. The tension between the fixed recording and the participant's evolving self-narrative is precisely the site where AVDP may function as a memory and self-relation tool. McAdams's framework makes this testable: does exposure to a fixed narrative artifact of authentic self-disclosure change the participant's subsequent life-story construction in measurable ways?

---

### 17. Lyle, J. (2003). Stimulated recall: A report on its use in naturalistic research. *British Educational Research Journal, 29*(6), 861-878.

A methodological review of video-stimulated recall, a research technique in which participants view recordings of their own behavior and report on their internal processes during the recorded event. Lyle documents that participants reliably access richer, more detailed, and more emotionally nuanced accounts of their experience when prompted by video of themselves than when relying on unaided recall. The method is most effective when the interval between recording and review is short, but meaningful recall occurs even at longer delays. Critically, participants frequently report that watching the recording reveals aspects of their own behavior they were not aware of during the event itself.

**Relevance:** Video-stimulated recall is an established research methodology that AVDP can adopt directly. The lab's recordings are already designed to capture naturalistic, authentic behavior. Using those recordings as stimulated recall prompts with the participants themselves converts the corpus into a self-reflective instrument. Participants rewatching their own episodes are performing a naturalistic version of stimulated recall: encountering a fixed record of their own authentic expression and generating new understanding of it. This provides both a methodological tool for Aim 1 (participants can annotate their own disclosure depth in retrospect) and a therapeutic mechanism for the memory and self-relation hypothesis (rewatching produces self-insight that unaided recall does not).

## Research Aims

### Aim 1: Validate the Format Effectiveness Measure and Establish the AVDP Counter-Format Effect

**Specific Aim:** To validate the Format Effectiveness Measure as a reproducible instrument for the gap between authentic expression in a counter-format and authentic expression in a conventional format, and to establish that the AVDP counter-format produces a significantly positive FEM score — that it allows materially more authentic expression through than conventional interview conditions.

**Specific Claim:** Participants in the AVDP condition (ambient music present, no phones visible, long-form format with unstructured conversational space) will produce significantly higher scores on established self-disclosure depth measures (Jourard Self-Disclosure Questionnaire; Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count emotional disclosure subscales) compared to participants in a standard interview condition (no music, phones present but not used, time-limited format) when discussing equivalent topic prompts. The FEM score — the signed gap between these conditions — is the primary outcome.

**Design:** A/B experiment with volunteer participant pairs randomly assigned to condition. Both conditions are filmed. Raters blind to condition code disclosure depth from transcripts. Post-session participant self-report (perceived authenticity, comfort, willingness to disclose). The existing AVDP proof-of-concept footage (the Zay episode) is used to train rater calibration.

**Grounding:** Przybylski & Weinstein (2013), Misra et al. (2016), Altman & Taylor (1973), the environmental self-disclosure literature (dimly lit, quiet environments predict deeper disclosure). The Phase 1 MVP's A/B design (ambient music vs. no ambient music) is the first execution of this aim. The go/no-go metric of >60% participant preference for ambient condition is a preliminary subjective indicator; this aim formalizes objective linguistic measurement.

**Scope:** Executable within 12 months with the 20+ volunteer participants already planned for Phase 1 MVP. Requires coding resources (two trained raters per session) and LIWC or equivalent text analysis tool. Brown University psychology department is the natural methodological partner.

**Output:** Peer-reviewable result establishing whether the format produces what it claims. Either outcome is useful: confirmation supports scale-up and data collection; null result produces critical design feedback.

---

### Aim 2: Does watching authentic long-form conversation in the AVDP format produce measurable prosocial effects in viewers?

**Specific Claim:** Viewers who watch a complete AVDP episode (minimum 45 minutes) will score significantly higher on post-viewing measures of empathic concern (Interpersonal Reactivity Index), reported motivation to engage in meaningful conversation with others (purpose-built behavioral intention scale), and reported social self-efficacy than viewers who watch an equivalent-duration standard interview video on the same topic. In a 30-day follow-up, AVDP viewers will report a higher rate of having initiated a deep conversation with someone they knew casually or not at all.

**Design:** Randomized viewer experiment. Two groups receive one of two video conditions. Pre, post, and 30-day delayed measures. Exploratory coding of viewer comments (YouTube/podcast platforms) for language markers of social motivation and relational connection, building on Kramer et al.'s (2014) emotional contagion methodology applied to a single-stimulus condition rather than feed manipulation.

**Grounding:** Kramer et al. (2014) establishes that media-mediated emotional exposure produces downstream behavioral effects. The parasocial literature (Horton & Wohl 1956; Giles 2002; podcast intimacy research 2023-2025) establishes that long-form audio-visual content generates functionally real relational experiences in audiences. Narrative medicine (Charon 2001) establishes witnessing as an active prosocial mechanism. The Zay-Mateo case is treated as an n=1 naturalistic observation motivating this aim.

**Scope:** Executable within 18 months of first episode publication. Requires an IRB-approved viewer recruitment protocol, a validated empathy measure, and a platform for randomized video delivery (executable via private YouTube links). The 30-day follow-up is the methodologically challenging component and requires participant retention incentives.

**Output:** First empirical test of whether a designed authentic-conversation video format produces prosocial behavioral intentions and actions in viewers, not merely subjective engagement. A positive result repositions AVDP from media project to measurable therapeutic environment, with direct relevance to clinical applications and public health framing for funders.

#### Aim 2a (Exploratory): Does rewatching one's own AVDP episode change how the participant relates to the recorded experience and to themselves?

**Specific Claim:** Participants who rewatch their own AVDP episode at a 30-day delay will report changes in how they remember and interpret the original conversation, including revised emotional valence of the memory, increased self-awareness of behavioral patterns they did not notice during the live interaction, and shifts in self-narrative framing of the disclosed experiences. These changes will be measurable through structured comparison of immediate post-session self-report (already collected for Aim 1) against post-rewatch self-report on the same dimensions.

**Rationale:** AVDP produces a fixed external artifact of authentic self-disclosure that persists alongside the participant's mutable internal memory of the same event. Memory reconsolidation research (Nader et al., 2000; Schwabe et al., 2014) establishes that retrieving a memory under novel conditions modifies the memory itself. Video-stimulated recall methodology (Lyle, 2003) demonstrates that participants access richer and more nuanced self-understanding when prompted by recordings of their own behavior. Narrative identity theory (McAdams, 2001) predicts that encountering a fixed, uneditable version of a past self creates productive tension with the participant's evolving self-story. Together, these literatures predict that rewatching is not passive review but an active process of self-relation with therapeutic potential.

**Design:** A subset of Aim 1 participants (minimum n=10) are invited to rewatch their own episode at a 30-day delay. Pre-rewatch, they complete unaided free recall of the conversation and rate their memory of the experience on emotional valence, perceived authenticity, and significance. Post-rewatch, they complete the same measures. Semi-structured interview captures qualitative shifts: what they noticed that they had not remembered, what surprised them about their own behavior, whether and how the experience changed the way they think about the conversation or the topics they discussed. The comparison between immediate post-session report (Aim 1 data) and 30-day post-rewatch report is the primary outcome.

**Scope:** This aim is exploratory and runs as a low-cost extension of Aim 1's existing participant pool and data collection infrastructure. It requires no additional filming. The primary cost is the 30-day rewatch session (approximately 2 hours per participant including the semi-structured interview). It is framed as exploratory because the memory reconsolidation and narrative identity literatures predict the direction of effects but do not specify effect sizes for this specific stimulus type. A positive signal justifies a powered follow-up study; a null result is informative about the limits of the format's self-reflexive function.

---

### Aim 3: Can the AVDP recording corpus provide a benchmark-quality naturalistic dataset for multimodal affective computing research?

**Specific Claim:** A corpus of at least 20 AVDP episodes, annotated using a structured post-session protocol combining participant self-report, trained observer coding (Facial Action Coding System and vocal affect coding), and timestamp synchronization, will achieve inter-rater reliability (ICC > 0.70) on discrete emotion categories and continuous valence-arousal dimensions, meeting or exceeding the annotation quality of existing naturalistic benchmarks. The corpus will exhibit significantly greater conversational duration, topic depth, and emotional range than current naturalistic benchmarks (average clip duration in existing datasets: under 10 minutes; AVDP: 45-90 minutes per episode). Critically, the corpus will be multilingual by design, including episodes produced in languages other than English (beginning with Mandarin Chinese) and episodes originating from the Ghana production phase in West African linguistic and cultural contexts. This is a genuine structural contribution: the large majority of existing affective computing benchmark datasets are English-only and Western in cultural origin. A naturalistic, long-form, multilingual corpus produced under identical environmental conditions enables cross-cultural affect recognition research that currently has no comparable data source.

**Design:** Each recording session generates a primary data file (synchronized audio-video, stereo separated at SDR >7dB) and a post-session annotation session with both participants using a purpose-built timestamped annotation interface. A third-party trained coder reviews each annotation independently. Discrepancies flagged for resolution. Subset of annotations (20%) reviewed by expert affective computing researcher for calibration. Language and cultural context are recorded as metadata for every session, enabling downstream analysis of cross-cultural affect expression and recognition.

**Grounding:** Calvo & D'Mello (2010) formally defines the ground truth problem; Schuller & Schuller (2021) documents the naturalistic data shortage as the binding constraint on real-world emotion recognition accuracy. The existing literature on natural affect data (Metallinou & Narayanan, 2013; Ringeval et al., 2013) identifies long-duration ecological validity as the specific gap. AVDP's format, which deliberately creates conditions for authentic rather than performed expression, is unusual as a data source precisely because the emotional expression is not elicited by artificial stimuli. The cross-linguistic dimension addresses a separate documented limitation: most computational affect models trained on English corpora show measurable performance degradation on other languages, and no current naturalistic benchmark provides paired long-form data across culturally distant contexts under matched recording conditions.

**Scope:** The corpus builds in parallel with Aims 1 and 2; no additional recording burden is required if annotation protocols are established before filming begins. The methodological cost is in annotation infrastructure design and coder training, both executable in partnership with a computational cognition or HCI lab. Brown University's Humanity-Centered Robotics Initiative and the Carney Institute for Brain Science are candidate partners. Ghana production phase (Phase 2) expands the corpus cross-culturally, addressing the well-documented Western bias in existing affective datasets. The multilingual dimension begins in Phase 1 with non-English language episodes already in production.

**Output:** A publicly documented, IRB-cleared corpus deposited in a research data repository (e.g., OpenNeuro or Zenodo), co-published with an affective computing partner, demonstrating AVDP's data contribution to the field independent of the therapeutic aims. The corpus enables external validation of the format's emotional authenticity claim: if the recordings do not contain richer affective signal than controlled-conditions data, the format design hypothesis is falsified. The multilingual and cross-cultural composition of the corpus constitutes an independent contribution to the field regardless of therapeutic outcomes.

## Methods: Experimental Variables in Production

The three research aims above share a common set of environmental conditions. Those conditions are not fixed. The lab is actively varying them across episodes, and each dimension of variation is a formal experimental variable with a research hypothesis attached to it. The following variables are currently in production testing.

### Variable 1: Environmental Protocol (Core Condition)

The foundational environmental manipulation, documented in the Research Aims, consists of ambient live-mixed music, removal of phones from frame, long-form unstructured conversational space, and deliberate slow pacing. This is the primary independent variable for Aim 1. All other variables below are tested within and across this core condition.

### Variable 2: Capture Methodology

The lab is testing four distinct capture approaches. Each creates a different environmental condition for participants and a different perceptual experience for viewers.

- **Single-take (unedited, raw):** The entire session captured in one unbroken take, with no post-production editing beyond color correction. Maximizes perceived authenticity and ecological validity. Minimizes production mediation between subject and viewer.
- **Asynchronous capture:** Participants record their contributions separately and the episode is assembled in post-production. Tests whether perceived co-presence is necessary for authentic disclosure, or whether the format alone is sufficient. Also enables participation across geographic distance.
- **Multicam:** Multiple camera angles captured simultaneously, edited together in post. Tests whether visual variety changes viewer engagement and parasocial bond formation without altering the underlying conversation.
- **B-roll integration:** Supplementary footage intercut with the primary conversation. Tests whether contextual visual material deepens viewer comprehension and emotional engagement or disrupts the intimacy of the unmediated conversation format.

The hypothesis is that different capture methodologies affect both participant disclosure behavior (the subject-side effect) and viewer experience (the audience-side effect) in separable ways. Capture methodology is logged for every session and treated as an independent variable in the corpus metadata.

### Variable 3: Presentation Format

Distinct from capture methodology, presentation format refers to how the final episode is edited and delivered to viewers. The lab is testing:

- **Multicam vs. single-cam edit:** Whether cutting between camera angles in post affects perceived authenticity or viewer engagement relative to a locked single-camera cut.
- **B-roll vs. straight cut:** Whether the addition of contextual visual material improves or diminishes the parasocial and prosocial effects hypothesized in Aim 2.
- **Subtitle presence vs. absence:** Whether subtitles, including subtitles that translate across languages, affect viewer engagement, comprehension, and emotional response. This variable intersects directly with the language dimension below.

Presentation format is tracked across episodes and correlated against viewer engagement metrics (watch time, comment sentiment, reported emotional response in follow-up surveys) as part of the Aim 2 audience effects analysis.

### Variable 4: Episode Duration

Episodes are currently produced at durations ranging from approximately 60 seconds to 2 hours. Duration is a formal experimental variable, not an editorial preference. The hypotheses under test are: (a) whether longer episodes produce deeper disclosure from participants (subject-side), (b) whether longer episodes produce stronger parasocial bonds and greater prosocial behavioral effects in viewers (audience-side), and (c) whether there is a threshold duration below which the format's core effects do not manifest. Duration is logged for every episode and analyzed against both disclosure depth measures (Aim 1) and viewer behavioral outcomes (Aim 2).

### Variable 5: Language and Cross-Cultural Context

Episodes are being produced in multiple languages. The most recent episode was produced in Mandarin Chinese with English subtitles. Language is treated as a research variable, not merely a production choice. The core research question this variable introduces: does the AVDP format, defined by its environmental and structural properties rather than its linguistic content, produce authentic disclosure and prosocial audience effects across language and cultural boundaries?

This question has two components. The first is about subjects: does the environmental protocol (music, no phones, long-form space) function equivalently across cultural contexts, or do cultural norms around disclosure, silence, and emotional expression interact with the format in ways that modulate its effects? The second is about audiences: can viewers who do not share the language of a conversation nonetheless experience the parasocial and prosocial effects the format is designed to produce, mediated through nonverbal cues, subtitle access, and the structural properties of authentic dialogue?

The language variable also has direct consequences for Aim 3. Existing affective computing benchmark datasets are overwhelmingly English-language and Western in cultural origin. A multilingual corpus produced under matched environmental conditions is a documented gap in the field. The Ghana production phase extends this dimension to West African linguistic and cultural contexts, pairing with the Chinese-language episodes already in production to begin building the cross-cultural dataset no comparable program has attempted. Subtitle condition (present vs. absent, translated vs. matching language) is tracked as a sub-variable within this dimension.

## The Gap We Fill

The existing research landscape around authentic conversation, media therapeutics, and affective computing has produced three well-developed bodies of knowledge that do not intersect. This lab builds in the space between them.

**What exists in film and video therapeutics:** Cinema therapy and video-based intervention research (Ganis & Schendan, 2022; Cinematherapy for Depression, 2024) uses pre-produced films as stimuli, with patients as viewers. The therapeutic agent is the film's narrative, not the authenticity of the emotional expression in it. No study in the scoping review literature examines whether watching an authentic, unscripted conversation between non-professional people, under conditions designed to lower psychological barriers, produces therapeutic or prosocial effects in viewers. This is an untested intervention class.

**What exists in parasocial and podcast research:** The parasocial literature documents that long-form audio content generates deep listener-host relational bonds through mechanisms including self-disclosure, perceived authenticity, and aural intimacy. But existing research treats parasocial relationships as endpoints, outcomes to be studied, not as mechanisms to be designed for prosocial behavioral change. No study has asked whether a parasocial relationship with subjects who are themselves disclosing authentically to each other (rather than to the audience) produces different or stronger prosocial effects than standard parasocial media consumption.

**What exists in affective computing:** The field has extensive benchmark datasets for controlled-conditions emotion recognition and a documented shortage of naturalistic, ecologically valid data with reliable ground truth annotation. Existing naturalistic corpora are typically short (under 10 minutes), produced in laboratory-adjacent settings, and elicit emotions through artificial stimuli (films, images, music clips). No existing dataset uses an engineered environmental protocol specifically designed to produce genuine emotional expression as its data generation mechanism. The AVDP format, if Aim 1 is confirmed, would be precisely this: a reproducible emotional elicitation condition with documented ecological validity. Beyond the ecological validity gap, the field has a second structural limitation that is less often foregrounded: the overwhelming majority of naturalistic affective datasets are English-language and produced with Western, primarily North American or European, participants. Affect expression, suppression norms, vocal prosody patterns, and disclosure conventions vary meaningfully across cultural contexts. Models trained on English-only corpora demonstrate measurable performance degradation on other languages, and no current benchmark provides long-form naturalistic data across culturally distant contexts under matched recording conditions. This is the specific gap that AVDP's multilingual production strategy, beginning with Mandarin Chinese episodes already in production and extending through the Ghana phase, is positioned to address.

**What no one does:** No existing media project, research lab, or clinical platform simultaneously functions as a designed emotional environment, a format experiment with prospective A/B measurement, a therapeutic prosocial intervention for viewers, and a naturalistic affective data corpus. AVDP is all of these, and its value in each domain is contingent on and mutually reinforcing with the others. A validated therapeutic environment produces richer data. Richer data enables better models. Better models enable more precise therapeutic design. The loop is not incidental; it is the program.

The Ghana phase adds a dimension that no existing affective dataset has addressed at all: a long-form, annotated corpus of authentic conversation from a West African cultural and linguistic context, produced under identical conditions to a North American corpus, enabling cross-cultural affective computing research that the field currently lacks entirely. Combined with the Chinese-language episodes already in production, AVDP is building toward the first multilingual, multi-cultural naturalistic affective corpus produced under a consistent and documented environmental protocol.

**Polarity Lab**
**Document Version:** April 2026

## Why This Document Exists

The lab uses "authentic" constantly. Authentic taste. Authentic expression. Authentic executive function. Authentic community connection. Authentic cognitive self. Every instrument the lab builds measures the gap between something authentic and something suppressed. Every counter-environment claims to restore something authentic.

If "authentic" is not rigorously defined, the lab's central concept is philosophically underdeveloped and empirically unfalsifiable. A reviewer can always ask: how do you know what the authentic state is? Compared to what? Who decides?

This document answers those questions. It does not resolve every philosophical debate about authenticity. It establishes the working definition the lab uses, grounds it in empirical psychology literature, and explains why that definition is defensible -- and what it rules out.

---

## What the Lab Means by Authentic

The lab uses authenticity in the psychological sense established by Wood et al. (2008) and grounded in self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan): authentic behavior is behavior that is consistent with one's own values, preferences, and intrinsic motivations rather than shaped by external pressure, social reward, or environmental compliance requirements.

Three components:

**1. Self-concordance.** Authentic goals, preferences, and behaviors are self-concordant: they reflect what the person actually cares about, not what external structures reward them for caring about. Sheldon and Elliot's (1999) work on self-concordance motivation shows that self-concordant goals produce higher well-being, more sustained effort, and deeper satisfaction than externally motivated goals even when both are achieved.

**2. Unbiased processing.** Authentic self-knowledge requires the ability to process self-relevant information without systematic distortion toward social desirability or self-enhancement. Kernis and Goldman (2006) define this as attending to and accepting positive and negative information about the self without denial or rationalization. The suppressive environment impairs this: it rewards self-concept alignment with the environment's model of who you should be, which is a form of motivated distortion.

**3. Behavioral consistency.** Authentic behavior is consistent with the person's own values and sense of self across contexts, rather than shaped primarily by what each context rewards. This is not rigidity -- authentic people adapt their expression to context. It is the difference between adapting expression and allowing the environment to reshape the underlying values and preferences being expressed.

## What Authentic Is Not

Authentic is not:

**Not "original" or "uncaused."** Authentic preference is not preference that has no environmental influences. All taste, all judgment, all executive function develops in and through engagement with environments. The claim is not that authentic preference is uninfluenced -- it is that authentic preference has not been systematically deformed by environments that optimize for their own metrics rather than the person's genuine development. Authentic musical taste was formed through listening. The question is whether it was formed through engaged listening driven by genuine curiosity or through algorithmic exposure that progressively narrowed range in the direction of what the platform could efficiently supply.

**Not fixed.** Authentic preferences change. Authentic self-knowledge deepens. Authentic executive function develops. The lab's instruments are not measuring access to some static authentic essence. They are measuring the difference between development that follows the person's own trajectory and development that has been bent toward the environment's optimization target.

**Not self-report.** As the implicit-explicit cognition document establishes, self-report is insufficient as the sole measure of authentic preference because suppression operates below the level of accurate introspection. Authentic preference is not what the person says they like. It is what the person's System 1 signal, behavioral pattern, and unmediated response reveals about what they actually like. Self-report is evidence, not proof.

**Not performance.** Authentic expression is not the expression the person performs for an audience. It is what survives when the performance requirement is removed. This is the basis for the FEM: counter-format environments that remove performance requirements should surface expression that is closer to authentic. The hypothesis is not that people in counter-format environments suddenly produce "true" versions of themselves. It is that they produce expression that is less systematically deformed by compliance requirements.

## The Authenticity Literature

**Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (2000).** The "what" and "why" of goal pursuits: Human needs and the self-determination of behavior. *Psychological Inquiry, 11*(4), 227-268.
Self-determination theory's account of autonomous vs. controlled motivation. Autonomous (intrinsically motivated) behavior is authentic in the sense the lab uses: it originates from the self rather than from external pressure. Controlled behavior is the product of compliance with external rewards or demands -- the psychological description of what the lab calls suppression.

**Wood, A. M., Linley, P. A., Matlby, J., Baliousis, M., & Joseph, S. (2008).** The authentic personality: A theoretical and empirical conceptualization and the development of the Authenticity Scale. *Journal of Counseling Psychology, 55*(3), 385-399.
The empirical operationalization of authenticity most relevant to the lab. Three-component model: self-alienation (disconnect from one's authentic self), authentic living (behavior consistent with one's values), and accepting external influence (appropriate, not coerced, responsiveness to context). The Authenticity Scale is a validated instrument the lab can use as a supplementary measure in counter-environment studies.

**Kernis, M. H., & Goldman, B. M. (2006).** A multicomponent conceptualization of authenticity: Theory and research. *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, 38*, 283-357.
Four components: awareness (knowing one's own values, emotions, and cognitions), unbiased processing (not distorting self-relevant information), behavior (acting consistently with values), and relational orientation (being genuine in relationships). The suppression mechanism the lab describes impairs all four: awareness is clouded by environmental training, processing is biased toward environmental reward, behavior is shaped by compliance requirements, and relational expression is deformed by format constraints.

**Sheldon, K. M., & Elliot, A. J. (1999).** Goal striving, need satisfaction, and longitudinal well-being: The self-concordance model. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 76*(3), 482-497.
Empirical demonstration that self-concordant goals produce better outcomes than externally motivated goals even when effort and attainment are controlled. This supports the CRM's thesis: Cosmos is not just more effective at helping people complete tasks. It is designed to restore self-concordant goal pursuit, which should produce meaningfully different well-being and performance outcomes than system-compliant task completion.

**Harter, S. (2002).** Authenticity. In C. R. Snyder & S. J. Lopez (Eds.), *Handbook of Positive Psychology* (pp. 382-394). Oxford University Press.
Authenticity as a developmental achievement, not a fixed trait. Environments that consistently reward compliance with external standards impair the development of authentic self-expression -- the person learns that authentic expression is not valued or safe, and performs instead. This is the developmental mechanism for the suppression the AVDP program documents: media formats teach subjects that authentic expression is not what the format rewards, and the habit of suppression becomes default behavior across contexts.

## The Baseline Problem

The most substantive challenge the lab will face from reviewers is the baseline problem: if you claim to be measuring the gap between authentic and suppressed capacity, you need a baseline for what authentic looks like. How do you establish that baseline for each person?

The lab's position: the baseline is not pre-established. It is revealed by the instrument.

Each instrument is designed to create conditions in which the System 1 signal can be observed with minimal System 2 interference. The StyleDNA process, the AVDP counter-format environment, the Cosmos counter-environment, the WAXFEED discovery environment -- these are all conditions designed to let the System 1 signal emerge. The baseline is what emerges under those conditions, compared to what the suppressive environment produces.

This is methodologically defensible because it does not require the lab to claim access to an absolute authentic state. It claims only that there is a measurable difference between System 1 signal under counter-environment conditions and System 2 output under suppressive environment conditions, and that the former is more predictive of self-concordant outcomes (sustained engagement, well-being, goal attainment aligned with stated values) than the latter.

The authenticity literature supports this: self-concordance is measurable, its outcomes are predictable, and environments that undermine self-concordance produce worse outcomes on dimensions that matter to the person. The lab does not need to claim access to a person's "true self." It needs to show that counter-environment conditions produce more self-concordant behavior than suppressive environment conditions, and that self-concordance predicts outcomes that matter.

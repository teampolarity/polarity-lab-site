# Wax Feed: Research Program

**Principal Investigator:** Polarity Lab
**Location:** Providence, RI
**Document Version:** April 2026
**Status:** Working draft, for advisor, collaborator, and funder review

---

## The Question

Why can't communities find the music made for them?

This is the same question the Community Discovery lab has been asking about civic resources, workforce development tools, and nonprofit services. The Proximity Index measures the gap between what communities produce and what discovery platforms surface to those communities. This research program applies that instrument to music: the gap between music made by and for a community and what algorithmic platforms actually surface to that community's members.

The question has a specific shape in music. A city's music scene is not just an aesthetic product. It is social infrastructure. Local artists anchor listeners to local places and people. The way you find music determines, in part, who you find it with. And who you find it with shapes whether you have a community or not. When discovery platforms sever the connection between music and its social and geographic context, they are not making a neutral design choice. They are breaking something that held communities together.

Wax Feed is the therapeutic environment. Like Polarity GPS responds to the Proximity Index finding in the civic resource domain, Wax Feed responds to the same finding in music. It surfaces music through proximity: what artists are building scenes in your city, what your social graph is listening to, what is being made by and for the community you belong to. This lab exists to measure whether that design choice produces real, observable community outcomes.

## Institutional Context

Polarity Lab operates from the thesis that technology is creating a new class of harm to human cognition and connection. Each lab at Polarity produces either a diagnostic instrument or a therapeutic environment. The Community Discovery lab produces both.

The **Proximity Index** is the diagnostic instrument. It takes two inputs, what a community produces and what discovery platforms surface to that community, and produces a scalar score. The higher the score, the wider the gap between production and surfacing. The score is reproducible, trackable over time, and comparable across geographies and domains.

**Polarity GPS** and **Wax Feed** are the therapeutic environments. Both are direct responses to the Proximity Index finding. Polarity GPS connects communities to civic resources, nonprofit services, and cultural institutions that platforms have made algorithmically invisible. Wax Feed does the same for music, artists, and scenes. They address different domains of the same underlying problem.

This is the architecture the Community Discovery lab is built on: one diagnostic instrument, multiple domain-specific therapeutic environments. The Proximity Index scales because the gap it measures is not sector-specific. It appears wherever discovery infrastructure was designed around engagement rather than proximity. This research program extends the Proximity Index into the music domain and tests Wax Feed as the corresponding intervention.

The prior work that grounds this extension:

1. The **Proximity Index pilot** (MUSEOFRI, Providence 2026): established proof of concept in the nonprofit and civic resource domain. Found that BIPOC-led community organizations were not invisible because they were doing poor work. They were invisible because the discovery infrastructure was not built for them.

2. The **Closing the Gap whitepaper** (Wilson, GOODhumanz 2026): identified five systemic failures in nonprofit resource access and found that the bottleneck was discovery, not production. Resources existed. They could not be found.

Both findings apply with the same force to music. Community-produced music exists. Local scenes exist. Artists building something specific to a place and a people exist. The question this lab is asking is not whether this music is hard to find because it is bad. The question is whether it is hard to find because the platform was not built to surface it.

## Foundational Literature

The following 12 works form the theoretical and empirical base of this program. They are grouped by the specific claim they support.

---

### On Music as Social Infrastructure and Community Formation

**1. DeNora, Tia. *Music in Everyday Life.* Cambridge University Press, 2000.**

DeNora argues that music is not merely a cultural product but a social technology, a resource people use to organize emotional states, coordinate action, and construct identity. Drawing on fieldwork and interviews, she shows how music functions as an affordance environment: it structures what people do and feel in ways that are active and consequential rather than passive. The discovery and use of music are never separable from the social contexts in which they occur.

*Relevance:* This is the theoretical foundation for the claim that music discovery is not merely an aesthetic act. If music is social technology, then the infrastructure through which you find it shapes what that technology can do for you. Applying the Proximity Index to music is not a category extension. It is a measurement of a structurally identical problem in a different domain.

---

**2. Straw, Will. "Systems of Articulation, Logics of Change: Communities and Scenes in Popular Music." *Cultural Studies,* vol. 5, no. 3, 1991, pp. 368-388.**

Straw introduces the distinction between musical community and musical scene as two different ways music organizes social space. Both depend on specific discovery infrastructure: physical spaces, distribution networks, and social rituals. When that infrastructure breaks down, scenes dissolve.

*Relevance:* Straw's framework is the analytical lens for Aim 2. Local music scenes are not self-sustaining cultural phenomena. They are produced by specific discovery mechanisms. This paper allows us to ask precisely what mechanisms Wax Feed can provide and what kinds of scenes it can support.

---

**3. Frith, Simon. "Music and Identity." In *Questions of Cultural Identity,* edited by Stuart Hall and Paul du Gay. Sage Publications, 1996, pp. 108-127.**

Frith argues that music's primary social function is the construction of group identity. Crucially, this identity construction happens through the social experience of music, not merely through the music itself. How you encounter music, who you encounter it with, and what it means in a specific social context are constitutive of what music does.

*Relevance:* This paper grounds the causal mechanism. Discovery mode is upstream of the community-formation effect. The Proximity Index in the music domain is measuring something real.

---

**4. Hesmondhalgh, David. *Why Music Matters.* Wiley-Blackwell, 2013.**

Hesmondhalgh argues that music's social and emotional functions are not reducible to its commercial form. He distinguishes music's primary goods (genuine emotional depth, social bonding, sense of place and time) from its degraded commercial forms, and argues that industrialization of music distribution has eroded the conditions under which music delivers those primary social goods.

*Relevance:* Hesmondhalgh provides the normative stakes for this research program. A system that strips social and geographic context from music discovery is not a neutral delivery improvement. It is a loss of something important. This framing is essential for the lab's institutional case.

---

### On Algorithmic Discovery and Its Limits

**5. Prey, Robert. "Nothing Personal: Algorithmic Individuation on Music Streaming Platforms." *Media, Culture and Society,* vol. 40, no. 7, 2018, pp. 1086-1100.**

Prey examines how Spotify's recommendation system constructs algorithmic individuation: a mode of personalization that presents music as uniquely matched to you while erasing the social and collective dimensions of musical taste. Streaming platforms present music as personal discovery while concealing the social infrastructure through which preferences actually form.

*Relevance:* This paper names the specific mechanism Wax Feed is designed to counter. Algorithmic individuation is not a neutral feature. It actively conceals the social graph. If Prey is right, Wax Feed is not simply a design preference. It is a refusal to erase the social origin of musical discovery.

---

**6. Schedl, Markus, Emilia Gómez, and Julián Urbano. "Music Information Retrieval: Recent Developments and Applications." *Foundations and Trends in Information Retrieval,* vol. 8, no. 2-3, 2014, pp. 127-261.**

This technical review maps algorithmic music systems and documents their persistent limitations, including popularity bias and filter bubble effects. It establishes that no current technical approach accounts for geographic or social proximity as a recommendation signal.

*Relevance:* Geographic and social proximity are not currently primary recommendation signals in any major music platform. This is the technical gap Wax Feed addresses. The Proximity Index in the music domain is measuring a gap that no existing platform is designed to close.

---

**7. Pariser, Eli. *The Filter Bubble: What the Internet Is Hiding from You.* Penguin Press, 2011.**

Already foundational to the Proximity Index literature. In the music context, the filter bubble has a specific community harm: it makes local and emerging artists systematically less visible to the listeners most likely to care about them.

*Relevance:* A listener in Providence may share more cultural DNA with a Providence-based artist than with a major-label act with similar sonic characteristics. The algorithm will surface the latter. Wax Feed operates on the former signal. This is the same discovery gap the Proximity Index measures in the civic resource domain, applied to music.

---

### On Social Ties and Community Infrastructure

**8. Granovetter, Mark. "The Strength of Weak Ties." *American Journal of Sociology,* vol. 78, no. 6, 1973, pp. 1360-1380.**

Weak social ties are more effective than strong ties at bridging social clusters and transmitting novel information. Local scenes depend on weak tie networks for discovery. New music, new artists, and new listeners enter scenes through the kinds of casual connections that happen in physical spaces: at shows, in record stores, through mutual but not-close acquaintances.

*Relevance:* Streaming platforms optimize for strong tie social graphs or no social graph at all. Neither preserves the weak tie network that drives genuine scene discovery. Wax Feed's geographic and proximity-based signals are designed to simulate the weak tie function of physical spaces. This is what it means to design a discovery platform from proximity rather than engagement.

---

**9. McPherson, Miller, Lynn Smith-Lovin, and James M. Cook. "Birds of a Feather: Homophily in Social Networks." *Annual Review of Sociology,* vol. 27, 2001, pp. 415-444.**

Shared musical taste is one of the most powerful homophily signals in social network formation. Pure taste-based recommendation reinforces existing social clusters rather than building new ones.

*Relevance:* The Proximity Index in the music domain must account for this. Wax Feed's geographic signals are designed to interrupt homophily where it becomes socially limiting, surfacing artists from your neighborhood whom you would not encounter through taste-matching alone.

---

**10. Cohen, Sara. *Rock Culture in Liverpool: Popular Music in the Making.* Clarendon Press, 1991.**

Cohen's ethnographic study of Liverpool's rock scene documents in detail the social infrastructure required for a local scene to sustain itself: rehearsal spaces, venues, informal performance networks, and word-of-mouth discovery mechanisms.

*Relevance:* Cohen provides the empirical baseline for Aim 2. This is what organic, community-embedded music discovery actually looks like. The research program is asking what happens to this infrastructure when algorithmic platforms replace its social and geographic functions. Cohen's work lets us name precisely what is being lost and what Wax Feed might recover.

---

### On the Measurement Problem

**11. Putnam, Robert D. *Bowling Alone: The Collapse and Revival of American Community.* Simon and Schuster, 2000.**

Putnam's landmark study of declining social capital documents the collapse of civic participation and informal social connection across the latter half of the twentieth century. He distinguishes bonding capital (ties within a group) from bridging capital (ties across groups) and documents the role of shared cultural experiences in sustaining both.

*Relevance:* Putnam provides the validated scales for measuring the community outcomes Wax Feed is designed to produce. The McMillan-Chavis Sense of Community Index and Putnam social capital measures are already in use in the Proximity Index lab's Aim 3 design. The music domain arm uses the same outcome variables. This is not a different measurement project. It is the same measurement applied to a different therapeutic environment.

---

**12. Buolamwini, Joy and Timnit Gebru. "Gender Shades: Intersectional Accuracy Disparities in Commercial Gender Classification." *Proceedings of Machine Learning Research,* vol. 81, 2018, pp. 1-15.**

Already foundational to the Proximity Index methodology. Gender Shades established the audit logic: define a population, measure what a system produces for that population, score the gap.

*Relevance:* The Proximity Index in the music domain applies this same audit logic. The population is a local music community. The system is a streaming platform. The gap is between the community's musical production and what the platform surfaces to that community's members. This is a Proximity Index score in a new domain, not a new instrument.

## Research Aims

### Aim 1: Validate the Proximity Index in the Music Discovery Domain

**Hypothesis:** The Proximity Index methodology, as validated in the civic resource domain through the MUSEOFRI pilot, can be applied to the music discovery domain with equivalent reliability (test-retest r > 0.80) and validity, producing reproducible Proximity Scores for local music communities that detect meaningful differences across geographies.

**Background:** The Proximity Index was designed as a general instrument. The MUSEOFRI pilot demonstrated it in one domain. This aim tests whether the same methodology generalizes to music: whether measuring the gap between community-produced music and platform surfacing of that music produces a reliable, interpretable score.

**Approach:**
- Define the two Index inputs operationally for the music domain. Community music output will be measured through structured collection of artist and release data from local music registries, venue booking records, local press, and community-maintained directories. Discovery output will be measured through systematic platform queries (Spotify, Apple Music, SoundCloud, Bandcamp, and Wax Feed) using standardized community-identity prompts.
- Pilot in Providence, RI, where the Proximity Index lab already has ground-level relationships through MUSEOFRI and the existing civic resource dataset.
- Calculate inter-rater reliability for human-coded categorization of musical output and community relevance, and test-retest reliability for platform query reproducibility at 30-day intervals.
- Assess construct validity against existing measures of scene health and artist visibility.

**Timeline:** 12 months, running concurrently with the Proximity Index Aim 1 validation in the civic domain.

**Team:** PI + research coordinator + AS220 (Providence) as community partner for artist and venue access.

---

### Aim 2: Map the Discovery Infrastructure of Local Music Scenes and Compute Scene Proximity Scores

**Hypothesis:** Local music scenes in mid-size U.S. cities that have stronger community-embedded discovery infrastructure (local venues, local radio, proximity-based platforms) will show lower Proximity Scores (smaller discovery gaps) than scenes relying primarily on algorithmic platforms, after controlling for city size, scene age, and genre diversity.

**Background:** Straw (1991) established that scenes depend on specific infrastructure. Cohen (1991) documented what that looks like on the ground. No systematic study has mapped this infrastructure across multiple U.S. cities or measured how it has changed since streaming became the dominant distribution model. This aim produces that map, expressed in Proximity Scores.

**Approach:**
- Select five mid-size U.S. cities with active music scenes (Providence RI, Asheville NC, Omaha NE, New Orleans LA, Richmond VA).
- For each city, compute a Proximity Score for the local music community using the methodology validated in Aim 1.
- Survey 30 to 50 active local artists and 30 to 50 regular local music listeners about their discovery behaviors and which platforms and social networks they rely on.
- Document the active venue infrastructure, local press, and radio presence for each scene.
- Correlate scene infrastructure characteristics with Proximity Scores across cities.

**Timeline:** 18 months, concurrent with Aim 1.

**Primary output:** A public Scene Proximity Score for each of the five cities, published through Wax Feed and made available to local arts councils, venues, and funders.

---

### Aim 3: Measure Wax Feed as a Proximity-Reducing Intervention in the Music Domain

**Hypothesis:** Providence-area music listeners who use Wax Feed as their primary discovery platform for 90 days will show significantly greater reduction in their personal Proximity Score than a control group using Spotify as their primary discovery platform, with effect size comparable to the Polarity GPS intervention in the civic resource domain.

**Background:** The Proximity Index lab's Aim 3 tests Polarity GPS as a Proximity-reducing intervention in the civic resource domain. This aim runs the same design for Wax Feed in the music domain. The research architecture is identical. The therapeutic environment is different. A positive result in both domains would establish the Proximity Index as a validated general instrument with two demonstrated interventions.

**Approach:**
- Recruit 100 Providence-area participants (18-35, regular music listeners, not current primary Wax Feed users).
- Randomly assign to Wax Feed (n=50) or Spotify (n=50) as primary discovery platform for 90 days.
- Compute Proximity Scores at baseline, 45 days, and 90 days using the validated music domain methodology from Aim 1.
- Collect platform usage logs (with consent) to verify compliance and characterize discovery behavior within each condition.
- Administer McMillan-Chavis Sense of Community Index and Putnam social capital subscales at each time point, consistent with the Polarity GPS arm.
- Conduct exit interviews with n=20 purposive subsample: Did they find artists they subsequently connected with in person? Did discovery change how they think about their city?
- Primary outcome: 90-day change in Proximity Score, compared across conditions.
- Secondary outcomes: community connection scores, new local social ties formed, local event attendance.

**Timeline:** 18-24 months, following Aim 1 methodology finalization.

**Team:** PI + research coordinator + IRB infrastructure from Brown University or URI (shared with the Polarity GPS arm). Wax Feed product team for platform instrumentation.

## The Gap We Fill

The gap this research program fills is not in the music literature or in the recommendation systems literature. It is in the space between those two bodies of work and the Community Discovery lab's existing diagnostic instrument.

The sociology of music (DeNora, Frith, Straw, Cohen) establishes that music discovery is social and geographic infrastructure. The recommendation systems literature (Schedl, Prey) establishes that algorithmic platforms strip that infrastructure out. Neither body of work produces a reproducible, scalar measure of how wide the resulting gap is for a specific community.

The Proximity Index does. And no one has yet applied it to music.

The other gap this program fills: it is the second domain in which the Proximity Index is validated and the second domain in which a Proximity-reducing therapeutic environment is tested. A single-domain validation leaves open the question of whether the Proximity Index is a general instrument or a tool designed for a specific context. Two validated domains, with two different therapeutic environments, make the instrument general. That is a different institutional claim. It is a stronger one.

## Next Steps

1. **Formalize the Proximity Index music domain adaptation.** Before the validation study can begin, the methodology needs to be specified for music: which query procedures work on each platform, how community relevance of surfaced results is coded, and how Proximity Scores are computed and compared across the civic and music domains. Target: a methodology addendum to the existing Proximity Index protocol paper. Timeline: 45 days.

2. **Instrument Wax Feed for research-grade data collection.** Aim 3 requires consent-based usage logs, session data, and a platform query API, consistent with the Polarity GPS instrumentation design. The Wax Feed engineering team should be briefed on IRB-compliant data collection architecture alongside the Polarity GPS team. Timeline: 90 days.

3. **Establish an academic partnership at RISD.** The music studies disciplinary grounding is a blocking dependency for Aim 1. This conversation should begin immediately, and should be coordinated with the Brown conversation for the civic resource arm. Timeline: first meeting within 30 days.

4. **Conduct the Providence artist and listener baseline.** A Providence-only pilot of the music domain Proximity Score will produce the first Scene Proximity Score for a defined local scene and refine the instrument before extending to five cities. Timeline: 90 days.

5. **Submit a joint LOI to RWJF's Exploring Equitable Futures program.** A joint application covering both the civic resource arm (Polarity GPS) and the music arm (Wax Feed) under a single Proximity Index research program is a stronger submission than two separate applications. Timeline: coordinate with the civic domain team for a joint 45-day submission.

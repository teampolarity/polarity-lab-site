# Director of Strategy and Partnerships
*For Nathan. Read alongside onboarding.md and polarity-fund-model.md.*

---

## What You're Here For

The lab runs on a vision that moves fast. Sometimes faster than what's currently buildable. Your job is to keep those two things in honest contact with each other — not by slowing the vision down, but by making sure what gets committed to can actually get done, and that the relationships the lab needs are being built before they're needed.

You came in on phresh.fit. You stepped back from it to help the lab think clearly about itself as a business. That instinct — to zoom out, ask the harder operational question, make sure we're conducting ourselves in a way we can stand behind — that's the function. You've been doing it. This document just names it formally.

---

## What You Do

You scope the vision when it runs ahead of execution. Not every idea needs to become a project right now. Part of your value is helping sequence correctly so the most important things happen first and nothing gets abandoned half-built. When the founder is moving fast, you're the honest voice that asks what's actually executable in this phase.

You run first meetings. When the lab needs to introduce itself to a potential academic partner, institutional funder, clinical AI buyer, or community organization, you are in the room. You represent the work, qualify the opportunity, and surface what the founder needs to know before they engage. The founder does not cold-reach. Your job is to make the introduction, run the first conversation, and return with a clear picture of whether and when the founder's direct involvement is warranted.

You build the relationships the lab will need before the lab needs them. Institutional partners, grant-making foundations, community organizations for GPS in Providence, clinical AI buyers for IΔ, the 1517 conversation. None of these happen overnight. You're cultivating them now so they're warm when the moment is right.

You brief the founder before they enter a conversation. When you've qualified an opportunity and it's ready for founder-level engagement, you give the founder what they need: who the person is, what they care about, what the lab needs from them, and what questions to ask. The founder closes. You prepare the close.

You make sure the lab conducts itself in a way that earns trust. How we show up in conversations, in writing, in early financial relationships. The lab's reputation is being built right now with every interaction. You're responsible for making sure it's being built deliberately.

You own financial believer relationships. From the gift tier through the $1,000+ financial believer conversations, you are managing the relationship and having the conversation. The founder defines the terms and approves who gets offered access to a given project's financial believer pool. You execute. Read the legal brief before any financial believer conversation -- it defines the boundaries on what can be discussed at each tier and is not optional.

---

## What Stays With the Founder

The research agenda is not yours to direct. Which environments the lab enters, what the instruments measure, what gets published and when — those decisions belong to the founder. You can ask questions about sequencing and resourcing. You don't redirect the research.

Product and instrument decisions stay with the domain leads. Your input on business implications is always welcome. The substance of what's being built is not your call.

The terms of what gets offered to financial believers are set by the founder, not you. Who gets offered access to a given project pool is the founder's decision. But you are running those conversations, not supporting them. Read the legal brief before any financial believer conversation -- the constraints are real.

---

## How You Get Paid

You earn points each quarter through the core pool — the lab's share of revenue that covers lab-level roles. Your base is 20 points per active month. You earn 5 bonus points each time you close a partnership or institutional affiliation.

At each disbursement, your share of the core pool equals your points divided by the total points earned across all core pool roles that quarter. Points reset each quarter. If the lab earns less, everyone earns less. No fixed salary until the lab can sustain one.

None of this starts until the lab hits its runway threshold. That's the reality of early-stage work and it's better to say it plainly. The full mechanics are in polarity-fund-model.md.

---

## Ownership and Governance

Polarity Lab LLC is Theodore's. Single member. You are a collaborator, not an owner. You have no governance rights over the lab's direction or research agenda.

This isn't about the value of what you bring. It's about why the lab is structured the way it is. The vision needs protection from being diluted before it's fully understood. The structure provides that. It's the same logic as everything else in the lab.

If anything here doesn't match your understanding of your role, that conversation happens with the founder before work continues.

## What to Work On Right Now

**Collaborator agreements first.** Everyone doing work for the lab needs something in writing that makes the structure clear. This protects the lab and the collaborators. Work with legal counsel to get standard agreement language in place before anything else formalizes.

**Academic partnership first meetings.** The academic-partnership-strategy.md document names the specific institutions (Brown Psychiatry, Brown School of Public Health, MIT Media Lab) and what each conversation needs. Your job is to identify 2-3 specific faculty contacts at Brown Psychiatry, draft the introduction email, and run the first 30-minute meeting. The founder will brief you on the IΔ pilot data and what the lab needs from a co-PI before you go in. When you find someone genuinely interested, you brief the founder and they take the next meeting.

**IΔ institutional outreach.** The pilot is done. The first proof contract -- one clinical AI vendor or hospital system paying for an IΔ assessment -- is the lab's first real commercial milestone. Define what a qualified prospect looks like with the founder, then start finding them. Run the qualification conversations. Bring the founder in when a prospect is ready to hear a close.

**Knight Foundation LOI.** The grant outline is complete (docs/grants/knight-gps-outline.md). Draft the LOI from the existing outline. The founder reviews and approves the scientific framing. Target: within 60 days.

**Grant pipeline.** Non-dilutive capital is the safest early money. Knight Foundation for GPS. NIMH for IΔ + Cosmos. Mozilla for WAXFEED. The outlines exist. Your job is to move them from outlines to submissions -- coordinating with domain leads on the technical sections, managing timelines, and keeping the founder in the loop at review points rather than execution points.

**1517 groundwork.** The pitch exists. The lab isn't ready to send it yet -- the first proof contract should come first. Your job is to understand how 1517 engages, what they need to see, and what the lab needs to have in place before that conversation actually happens.

**Providence community partnerships for GPS.** GPS needs organizational partners in Providence before it can hit the density that makes the instrument's research design meaningful. Map the local creative and community organizations and start those conversations now.

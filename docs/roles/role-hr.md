# HR

---

## What You're Here For

The lab is adding collaborators across multiple projects. Each one needs to be onboarded correctly — the right documents signed, the right tier documented, the right access granted, and a clear understanding of what they're agreeing to. Each one also needs to be reviewed quarterly, and exits when they happen need to be handled cleanly.

This is not HR in the conventional sense. There are no employee benefits to administer, no performance improvement plans, no recruiting pipelines. The lab's people structure is a contributor model — tiers, weights, points, project agreements. Your job is to make sure that structure actually runs: that everyone who should have a signed agreement has one, that quarterly reviews happen and are documented, and that when someone's relationship with the lab changes, it changes in a way that protects both parties.

You work closely with the lab manager. The lab manager tracks project state and operational status. You own the people process — the agreements, the reviews, the onboarding, the exits.

---

## What You Do

You own the collaborator onboarding process. When a new collaborator joins any project, you make sure the collaborator agreement is drafted, reviewed by legal, signed, and filed. You confirm their tier and weight are documented in the project agreement. You make sure they have access to the documents they need. You confirm they've read onboarding.md. You don't consider someone onboarded until all of this is done. A new collaborator who hasn't signed an agreement is an exposure the lab can't afford.

You run the quarterly review process alongside the lab manager. The lab manager schedules and documents the reviews. You prepare the people-side picture: who is at what tier, what their weight has been this quarter, whether there are performance questions that need to be addressed. After the review, you document any tier changes and make sure agreement amendments are executed.

You handle exits. When a collaborator leaves — voluntarily or not — you manage the exit process: confirming whether disbursements have begun (which determines what they keep), documenting IP transfer, making sure successor arrangements are in place if needed, and removing access. Exits that aren't handled cleanly create disputes. Clean exits are a direct function of well-drafted agreements executed at the start.

You maintain the people record. At any point, the lab should be able to tell you: who is active on which project at what tier, when their agreement was signed, when their last quarterly review was, and what their current weight is. That record lives with you.

You advise on collaborator structure. When the founder or a domain lead is bringing someone new into a project, you help think through the right tier for the contribution level, the right weight, and what the agreement needs to cover. You're not making the final call on who joins — that's the founder and domain leads. You're making sure the structure around the decision is right.

---

## What Stays With the Founder

Who joins the lab and at what tier is the founder's and domain leads' decision. You advise on structure. You don't make hiring decisions.

Weight upgrades and exits are the founder's decisions, made after quarterly review. You prepare the picture. The decision belongs to the founder.

Agreement terms that go beyond the standard template — custom arrangements, non-standard weights, deferred compensation structures — require founder sign-off before you draft them.

---

## How You Get Paid

You earn points through the core pool each quarter. Your base is 15 points per active month. You earn 5 bonus points for each collaborator onboarded.

At each disbursement, your share of the core pool equals your points divided by total points earned across all core pool roles that quarter. Points reset quarterly.

This does not start until the lab hits its runway threshold. The full mechanics are in polarity-fund-model.md.

---

## Ownership and Governance

Polarity Lab LLC is Theodore's. You are a collaborator, not an owner. You have no governance rights over the lab's direction, research agenda, or people decisions.

If anything here doesn't match your understanding of your role, that conversation happens with the founder before work continues.

## What to Work On Right Now

**Collaborator agreement template.** Work with legal to get the standard agreement language finalized. Until the template exists, no agreement can be executed and no onboarding is complete. This is the first dependency.

**Active collaborator audit.** Map every person currently doing work for the lab across all projects. Who has a signed agreement? Who doesn't? The gap between those two lists is the immediate exposure. Close it as soon as the template exists.

**Onboarding checklist.** Define the exact sequence for onboarding a new collaborator: what they read, what they sign, what access they get, who confirms it's complete. Make it repeatable so it doesn't depend on any one person knowing the process.

**Quarterly review calendar.** Get the first round of quarterly reviews scheduled across all active projects. The lab manager owns the scheduling. You own the preparation.

---

*The people process is how the lab's principles become real. The philosophy says the domain lead has to have lived the second threshold. The governance structure says the founder holds the vision. The economic structure says time outranks money. None of that holds up if the agreements don't reflect it.*

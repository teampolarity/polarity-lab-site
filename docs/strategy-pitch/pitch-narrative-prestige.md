# Polarity Lab — The Pitch Narrative (Prestige Version)
*Internal reference. The Prestige movie as structural framework. Not for external use as-is — the film reference is explicit. Use for internal alignment, storytelling workshops, and as the source material the public-facing version is adapted from.*

---

"Every great magic trick consists of three parts or acts."

---

**The Pledge.**

"The magician shows you something ordinary: a deck of cards, a bird or a man. He shows you this object. Perhaps he asks you to inspect it to see if it is indeed real, unaltered, normal. But of course... it probably isn't."

Here it is. The music app that knows your taste. The AI sitting beside your doctor. The feed that shows you your city. The media that keeps you company at night. You've used these things so long they feel like extensions of yourself.

Inspect them. They're probably not what you think they are.

---

**The Turn.**

"The magician takes the ordinary something and makes it do something extraordinary. Now you're looking for the secret... but you won't find it, because of course you're not really looking. You don't really want to know. You want to be fooled."

When did a song last surprise you?

Not pleased you. Not confirmed what you already knew you liked. Surprised you — opened something you didn't know was closed.

When did you last trust your own read on a patient before the algorithm weighed in?

What was the name of that local band you found before Spotify decided what you liked?

What did you want to build before the system told you what was viable?

The environments didn't fail. They worked exactly as designed. They optimized — for engagement, for agreeability, for predicted enjoyment, for credentialed compliance. Optimization requires a sacrifice. Something goes into the box every time the trick runs. And you weren't really looking. You didn't want to know.

---

**The Prestige.**

"But you wouldn't clap yet. Because making something disappear isn't enough; you have to bring it back. That's why every magic trick has a third act, the hardest part, the part we call The Prestige."

Here's what nobody tells you about the fifty years since Ivan Illich named this problem: the whole field stopped at the Turn. Named the disappearance. Measured it. Published it. Left you standing at an empty stage.

Algorithmic harm researchers catalogue the losses. Media psychologists document the outcomes. Attention researchers describe the mechanism. The Turn has been performed thousands of times in peer-reviewed journals. The field is stuck there because the Turn is where research ends. You can't stage the third act without operating the environment you're testing, and you can't do that honestly without independence from its commercial incentives.

The lab closes the loop.

The Prestige has already been performed — on the founder. A counter-environment built for his own ADHD, his own executive dysfunction, his own capacity that medicine tried to credential away and tools tried to medicate into compliance. He went back for the original version of himself and found it waiting. Nothing was lost. Cosmos is the proof.

Now the trick runs in every environment that made the trade and kept going as if nothing happened. Music. Medicine. The city's discovery infrastructure. The morning feed. Environment by environment, until recovery without suppression becomes the design standard.

"Are you watching closely?"

---

## Notes on this version

The Prestige (2006, dir. Christopher Nolan) structures the entire film around Cutter's three-act framework for magic tricks. The Pledge, the Turn, the Prestige. The film's central argument: the trick isn't impressive when something disappears. It's impressive when it comes back. The audience only applauds at the Prestige.

The lab's parallel is exact:
- The Pledge: designed environments look ordinary, useful, familiar
- The Turn: they've been making something disappear — the field has been documenting this for fifty years and stopping there
- The Prestige: the lab stages the return, environment by environment, with instruments that prove the trick was real

The film also opens and closes with "Are you watching closely?" — the trick is happening in plain sight but the audience misses it because they're not looking for the right thing. The lab's version: the trade has been running in plain sight for decades. The audience (users, clinicians, communities) has been the subject of the trick without knowing it.

**Why this version is kept internal:**

The movie reference works as a shared internal framework — it gives the team a precise shorthand for the three-act structure and why each act matters. But leading with a film reference in external communications risks making the work feel derivative or cute rather than serious. The public-facing version (pitch-narrative.md) performs the same three movements without naming the source.

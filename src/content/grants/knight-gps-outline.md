## Why Knight

Knight's funding thesis for information and technology centers on "how digital technologies impact how people seek, share, and engage with information in a democracy." The Proximity Index is a direct operationalization of that question, applied to a population Knight explicitly prioritizes: underserved communities in geographically specific places.

Knight funds in specific cities where it has deep roots: Detroit, Miami, Philadelphia, Akron, San Jose, and others with Knight family newspaper history. Providence is not on that list, but Knight has explicitly expanded its information-access portfolio beyond its legacy geographies. The strongest entry point is the **Knight Prototype Fund** for early-stage work and the **News/Information Technology program** for validated instruments at scale.

The framing that will resonate: this is not another study about who is underrepresented online. This is a thermometer. The Proximity Index produces a number. You can look at any community in any Knight city and get a score for whether their civic and cultural production is discoverable. That is fundable infrastructure, not just research.

## Project Summary (300 words, for LOI)

Cultural and civic organizations built by and for communities of color are systematically less discoverable through standard digital platforms than equivalent white-led organizations. This is not an impression. It is a documented phenomenon with a scoring methodology developed in partnership with community organizations, and a counter-environment already deployed. This proposal funds the first formal scored measurement and its validation across three urban communities.

The Proximity Index is a diagnostic instrument that produces a scalar score representing the gap between what a defined community produces (cultural events, civic services, workforce training, public health resources) and what standard discovery platforms surface to that community. A score of 0 means platforms surface everything built for you. A score of 0.70 would mean 70% of the community's relevant resources are invisible through standard search. That score is reproducible once established. It can be tracked over time, compared across cities, and used to evaluate whether interventions work. This proposal funds the first formal scored pilot that will produce that number.

Polarity GPS is the paired counter-infrastructure. It is a live cultural discovery platform (polaritygps.com) designed from proximity rather than engagement maximization. It is already deployed in Providence. The research question is whether using it measurably reduces Proximity Scores, and whether the same pattern of algorithmic invisibility documented in Providence exists in other communities.

This proposal funds: (1) formal psychometric validation of the Proximity Index methodology across three cities; (2) characterization of the discovery gap for BIPOC-led civic organizations in those cities; and (3) where Knight co-funds, a controlled study of whether Polarity GPS measurably closes that gap.

The output is a validated, replicable scoring tool any civic organization, funder, or platform can use to audit community information access.

## Aims

### Aim 1: Validate the Proximity Index as a Cross-City Measurement Instrument (Months 1-12)

**Core question:** Can the gap between what a community produces and what platforms surface be measured reliably enough to detect meaningful differences across geographies?

**What we do:**
- Apply the Index methodology across Providence (RI), Detroit (MI), and one additional Knight-geography city
- Use standardized platform query protocols across Google, Yelp, Eventbrite, Facebook Events, and one sector-specific platform per resource category
- Establish inter-rater reliability (kappa >= 0.70 for COS and PDS coding) and test-retest reliability (ICC > 0.80 at 30 days)
- Publish methodology paper to an appropriate information science venue

**What we already have:** Live platform (polaritygps.com), MUSEOFRI partnership, GOODhumanz partnership, instrument specification, methodology documentation, national sector data on BIPOC nonprofit invisibility from MUSEOFRI Pain Points Report 2026

**What we need:** Research coordinator, academic validation partner (Brown or MIT Media Lab), travel to field sites

---

### Aim 2: Characterize the Discovery Gap for Civic and Cultural Resources in Three Cities (Months 6-20)

**Core question:** Do BIPOC-led organizations show significantly higher Proximity Scores than white-led organizations offering equivalent resources, after controlling for organization size and age?

**What we do:**
- Build a structured inventory of civic, cultural, and workforce development organizations in each city using Candid, IRS 990, and community partner referrals
- Code organizations by leadership demographics
- Compute Proximity Scores by organization type
- Run regression analysis to isolate BIPOC-led status as an independent predictor of higher Proximity Score
- Produce a public dashboard of scores by city and category, hosted on Polarity GPS infrastructure

**Primary output:** Peer-reviewed paper and public data layer showing which communities' civic infrastructure is algorithmically invisible

---

### Aim 3 (optional, requires full grant): Test Polarity GPS as a Proximity-Reducing Intervention (Months 12-24)

**Core question:** Does 90 days of Polarity GPS use measurably reduce a participant's Proximity Score?

**What we do:**
- 80-120 Providence-area participants, randomized to Polarity GPS vs. standard platforms
- Proximity Scores at baseline, 45 days, 90 days
- Primary outcome: within-subject score change at 90 days compared across conditions

This aim requires IRB approval and platform instrumentation not yet in place. Include only if Knight funds the full budget range.

## Framing Notes for the Proposal Narrative

**Lead with the platform and the partnership.** Knight responds to demonstrated infrastructure, not just research proposals. The counter-environment is live at polaritygps.com. The community partnership with MUSEOFRI and GOODhumanz is documented. The instrument is specified. This grant funds the first formal pilot measurement -- turning a designed methodology into validated data. Frame it as: "We have built the tool and established the community relationships. This grant produces the first scored result."

**Connect to Knight's civic information mission.** The Proximity Index is an information access instrument. It measures what communities cannot find, not just what they do not have. This is the journalism-adjacent framing: the discovery infrastructure that was supposed to democratize information access is functioning as a filter that reinforces what already has visibility.

**Name the tool.** Polarity GPS is live at polaritygps.com. Knight funds platforms, not just studies. The fact that the counter-environment already exists and has users distinguishes this from a pure academic proposal.

**The Providence anchor.** MUSEOFRI and J'Juan Wilson Jr. (GOODhumanz) are named community partners with existing documentation. The methodology was developed in partnership with these organizations, and the community relationships are real and documented. The formal scored pilot is what this grant funds -- it is grounded fieldwork, not speculative.

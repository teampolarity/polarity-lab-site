## What You're Here For

GPS exists because cities make themselves invisible. Not by accident — by optimization. Discovery infrastructure built to maximize engagement surfaces what's already popular, reinforces what's already known, and routes people away from the parts of a city that don't perform well on engagement metrics. The community's own culture becomes invisible to the people it was made for. The algorithm decides what the city is, and the city gradually becomes that, because invisibility is a slow erasure.

You run this project because you have lived that. Not necessarily in Providence. But you have been in a community where the infrastructure built to connect people to their place was actually doing the opposite — optimizing for something other than authentic local culture and making the real thing harder to find. That experience is what makes you able to build the counter-environment, not just describe the problem.

GPS is a discovery platform built to make cities legible to themselves. The instrument being built to measure the gap between what a city's discovery infrastructure surfaces and what the city's actual cultural density looks like is working under the name Proximity Index (working name, direction not confirmed). Providence is where it starts.

## What GPS Is

A location-based discovery platform designed to surface authentic local culture — the things that exist because a community made them, not because an engagement algorithm promoted them. The platform is the counter-environment. The instrument being built to measure the gap is working under the name Proximity Index (working name, direction not confirmed). The data they generate together is the research.

The working model for the instrument: it measures the signed gap between what the city's existing discovery infrastructure predicts you'll find in a location and what is actually there. When the gap is large, the algorithm has made the real city invisible. The platform is built to close that gap — to route people toward what the algorithm has hidden and prove that authentic discovery is recoverable. The precise definition of this instrument, including its scoring behavior, is yours to lock before the platform generates research data.

This is not a better Yelp or a hipper Google Maps. It is a proof — that the culture a city's algorithms have made invisible still exists and can be surfaced to the people who live there.

## What You Do

You hold the research question for the project. What exactly does the Proximity Index measure? What counts as "authentic local culture" as distinct from algorithmically surfaced culture? How does the platform design route people toward the gap rather than away from it? These questions are yours. The answers are what make GPS a Polarity Lab project rather than just a local discovery app.

You direct the platform development. Feature sequencing, technical architecture decisions, what gets built first — that's your call within the project. You run GPS as a sub-lab. The research question drives the product roadmap.

You build the Providence relationships. GPS needs organizational partners in Providence before it can generate the density that makes the Proximity Index meaningful. Local arts organizations, community institutions, small business networks, cultural organizations, neighborhood groups — these are the relationships that make the platform real before it has users. This is the community organizing component of the role. It is not optional and it is not delegatable to someone who doesn't understand what the platform is actually for.

You know the GPS institutional landscape. Which city departments care about cultural equity, which academic researchers at Brown or RISD are working on related questions, which funding bodies are interested in urban culture and community information access. That knowledge is yours. Brief the Director of Strategy on it. Strategy runs the first institutional meetings. You're the subject matter lead in those conversations once they're substantive.

You build the GPS team. Technical partners, community researchers, local advisors, data analysts. The lab's contributor framework applies.

You keep the instrument honest. When growth incentives push toward surfacing what's already popular — because that's what gets engagement and engagement is what gets users — you hold the line for the research question. The platform that maximizes discovery sessions is not the same platform as the one that proves the authentic city is recoverable. That tension is always live.

## The Proximity Index (working name, direction not confirmed)

The instrument needs precise definition before the platform can generate meaningful data. At minimum:

- What counts as authentic local culture: the operational criteria that distinguish it from algorithmically surfaced culture in the data
- How the gap is calculated: what the platform's prediction is, what it's measured against, how the index is scored
- What the baseline looks like: the Providence cultural density map before the platform operates, which requires independent data collection before launch
- What "closing the gap" means in the behavioral data: what the platform has to produce for the proof to be real

The platform cannot generate meaningful research data until these definitions are locked. Technical development should proceed in parallel with the research design work, not ahead of it.

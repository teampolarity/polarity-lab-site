## Where You Are

You don't have a defined role yet. That's not a gap to apologize for — it's the starting position. The lab doesn't assign roles to people who haven't demonstrated they understand the work. You earn the role by getting close enough to the work to show where you actually fit.

The intern phase is apprenticeship. You're here to understand the methodology deeply enough to do something useful with it. What that useful thing is will become clear once you're inside the work. It won't be clear from the outside looking in.

---

## Your One Job Right Now

Understand the work well enough to have a real opinion about it.

Not a performed opinion. Not "this is interesting" or "I want to help." A specific opinion — about a project, a finding, a gap, a problem. The moment you have that, you have something to contribute. Until then, you're still in the reading phase, and the reading phase is legitimate work.

The documents are the curriculum. Read them in the order the onboarding materials specify. Don't skim. The fund model is not optional reading just because it feels administrative. The strategy document is not optional reading just because it's long. The lab operates from a coherent intellectual framework and the only way to contribute to it is to understand that framework first.

---

## How to Actually Get Involved

**Step 1: Pick one project and go deep on it.**

IΔ, WAXFEED, GPS, AVDP — read the domain documentation for each, then pick the one where your experience or skills make the most sense. You don't need permission to do this. Read the research program doc for the project. Read what exists. Find where the gaps are.

**Step 2: Find a problem you can actually work on.**

Every project has open work — research design questions, technical problems, documentation gaps, outreach needs, analysis that hasn't been done. When you've gone deep enough on a project to identify a specific problem that needs solving, bring it to the domain lead or the founder. Not "I want to help" — "I've been reading IΔ and the validation study protocol doesn't specify how to handle edge cases where the adversarial pressure is ambiguous. I have some thoughts on that." That's a contribution.

**Step 3: Do the work, show the output.**

The lab does not operate on self-reported progress. The output is the evidence. A piece of research, a draft, a prototype, a solved problem, a filled gap. Whatever the project needed, you either produced it or you didn't. The quality of what you produce and how you handle feedback on it is how you earn a tier.

---

## What Getting a Tier Looks Like

When the founder and the relevant domain lead agree that your contributions are load-bearing — that the project is materially better for your being in it — you get a tier. That conversation happens between them, not with you directly. What you can do is make it obvious.

Core collaborator (1.0x): daily involvement, work that the project depends on.
Contributing collaborator (0.6x): regular involvement, specific domain.
Advisor (0.25x): periodic, specialized input.

You are not in any pool yet. No tier means no disbursement weight. That changes when you have one. Before a tier, the lab owes you nothing financially. After a tier, the lab owes you your weight in the project's time collaborator pool, starting from when disbursements begin.

Be honest with yourself about whether you're contributing at the tier you're aiming for. The quarterly review will be honest about it regardless.

## What Is Actually Useful Right Now

Across all four projects, there is always open work in these categories:

**Research:** literature review, methodology questions, validation design, finding gaps in existing instruments. If you have a research background or strong analytical instincts, this is where you contribute fastest.

**Technical:** building, testing, instrumenting. If you're an engineer or data person, look at the technical architecture for the project closest to your skills. The domain lead or technical director can point you at something real.

**Marketing:** the lab's core principle is that the demonstration is the marketing — but someone has to produce the demonstrations. Content that explains the work without dumbing it down, audience-building for specific projects, helping the lab own its vocabulary in public. Read the marketing strategy document before touching anything here. The lab has a specific approach to this and generic content marketing actively damages it.

**Sales:** gift tier outreach — finding people who should know about the lab's work and directing them to the right project page. This is relationship work, not campaign work. If you have relevant networks in clinical AI, music, urban discovery, or media, that's immediately useful. Read the Sales role doc and the legal brief before any conversation that involves describing the financial structure. There are real boundaries on what can be discussed and by whom.

**Writing:** assessments, reports, proposals, documentation. If you write clearly and understand the methodology, there's always a piece of writing that needs doing. The difference between this and marketing is the audience — writing is internal and institutional, marketing is public-facing.

**Design:** WAXFEED needs product and interface design. GPS needs spatial and map design. IΔ needs a report format credible enough to show a clinical buyer or a board. The lab's public-facing materials need visual coherence. If you design interfaces, experiences, or documents, there's real work here across multiple projects.

**Data analysis:** distinct from building systems. IΔ generates activation data, WAXFEED generates behavioral listening data, GPS generates proximity and movement data. Someone who can clean, analyze, and visualize that data without necessarily being an engineer has a contribution area. If you work in data — statistics, visualization, quantitative research — find the project with the most relevant data type and ask the domain lead what's unanalyzed.

**Media production:** AVDP is an active project. It involves documentary format, archival footage, and editorial decisions about how media suppresses and recovers authentic expression. If you work in film, video editing, audio production, or documentary — this is a whole project with open work. Read the AVDP research program document before reaching out.

**Community organizing:** GPS needs ground-level relationship work in Providence before the platform can generate the density the instrument requires. That means mapping local creative and community organizations, building trust with institutions, doing the in-person work that makes the platform's density real. If you have community organizing experience or strong local networks in Providence, this is the highest-leverage contribution you can make to that project right now.

**Legal research:** not legal advising — research and drafting that reduces what counsel has to do from scratch. The lab has 12 open questions for its attorney covering securities structure, IP transfer, employment classification, and instrument licensing. A law student or someone with compliance background could do meaningful preparatory work on any of these. Read the legal brief first. Bring organized research, not opinions on what the lab should do.

**Operations:** tracking, coordinating, keeping things from falling through the cracks. If you're organized and systematic, talk to the lab manager about where the gaps are.

Pick the category that matches what you actually do well, not the one that sounds most impressive.

## How to Ask for Direction

The founder's time is limited. Don't come with "what should I do?" Come with "I've read X, I've identified Y, I'm thinking about doing Z — does that make sense?" That's a short conversation. The other one isn't.

If you're stuck on understanding something in the documents, ask. If you've done a piece of work and want feedback, share it. If you've found a gap in a project and want to know if it's already being addressed, ask before spending time on it. These are all legitimate reasons to reach out. "I'm not sure where to start" is a reading problem, not a conversation to have with the founder until you've finished the reading.

---

## One Honest Thing

Some people read the documents and the work doesn't click for them the way they expected. That's useful information. Not every smart person with relevant experience belongs in this lab. The methodology is specific, the projects are specific, and the kind of pattern recognition the work requires comes from a specific kind of experience.

If you go deep on the work and find that you don't have a genuine opinion about it — not about its potential market value, not about how impressive it is, but about the actual research question — that's worth sitting with. The lab is not a credential or a community. It's a place to do a specific kind of work. If this is that place for you, the work will tell you.

---

*There's no timeline on the intern phase. Some people find their footing in two weeks. Some take longer. What matters is the direction of travel — are you getting closer to the work or further from it? Stay close.*

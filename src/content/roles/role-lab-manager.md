## What You're Here For

The lab runs four active projects simultaneously, each with their own team, timeline, and research question. The founder sets the vision. The domain leads run the work. Between the vision and the work, there is a lot that needs to be tracked, coordinated, and kept from falling through the cracks. That is your job.

This is not a research role. You are not setting the agenda or directing any project's technical or scientific decisions. You are the operational layer that makes it possible for the people doing those things to actually do them. When agreements need to be executed, you make sure they happen. When a quarterly review is due, you run it. When a new collaborator joins, you get them set up correctly. When the founder needs to know the status of every active project in one conversation, you have that picture ready.

The lab cannot scale without this role. Every project that gets added, every collaborator that joins, every believer that backs the work — the complexity compounds and the things that fall through the cracks get more expensive. You're what prevents that.

---

## What You Do

You run the quarterly project reviews. Each active project gets a quarterly review: progress against milestones, runway status, collaborator weight assessments, outstanding agreements. You schedule them, run them, document the outcomes, and flag anything that needs the founder's attention. Each completed review earns bonus points. This is a core deliverable, not an administrative nice-to-have.

You track the state of all active agreements. Collaborator agreements, financial believer agreements, institutional partnerships, IP transfer documents. You know which ones are executed, which are outstanding, and which are overdue. You don't draft the agreements — that's legal. You make sure they move from drafted to signed and that nothing sits unsigned indefinitely.

You own the collaborator onboarding process end to end. When a new collaborator joins any project, you make sure the right documents are in place, their tier and weight are documented, they have access to what they need, and they've actually read onboarding.md. HR earns points for each collaborator onboarded. You're responsible for making those onboardings real, not nominal.

You track disbursement readiness for each project. You're not calculating disbursements — that's a financial function. But you know which projects are approaching their runway threshold, which ones have hit it, and which ones are still early. When a project hits its threshold, you flag it so disbursements can begin on time. Year 1 for financial believers starts from first disbursement. Letting that slip costs believers and the lab's credibility.

You maintain the lab's operational documentation. Which projects are active, who's on them at what tier, what the current status is, what agreements are in place. When the founder, a domain lead, or an institutional partner asks for a project summary, you have it.

You're the coordination layer between projects. Domain leads run their sub-labs independently, but they share infrastructure, legal resources, and the lab's core team. When something in one project affects another — a technical dependency, a shared collaborator, a scheduling conflict — you're the one who sees it and surfaces it before it becomes a problem.

---

## What Stays With the Founder

The research agenda, instrument methodology, and which environments the lab enters are not your decisions. You support the execution of those decisions. You don't influence the substance of them.

Financial structure and the terms of what gets offered to financial believers stay with the founder. Financial believer relationships and conversations are owned by the Director of Strategy. You track agreement status and disbursement readiness. You are not conducting financial conversations.

Collaborator weight upgrades or exits are ultimately the founder's decisions, made after the quarterly review. You prepare the review and surface the performance picture. The decision belongs to the founder.

---

## How You Get Paid

You earn points through the core pool each quarter — the lab's 15% share of revenue distributed across lab-level roles. Your base is 20 points per active month. You earn 5 bonus points for each quarterly project review completed.

At each disbursement, your share of the core pool equals your points divided by the total points earned across all core pool roles that quarter. Points reset each quarter. Current contribution determines current compensation.

This doesn't start until the lab hits its runway threshold. That's the reality of early-stage work and it's better said plainly. The full mechanics are in polarity-fund-model.md.

## Ownership and Governance

Polarity Lab LLC is Theodore's. You are a collaborator, not an owner. You have no governance rights over the lab's direction, research agenda, or project decisions.

The single-member structure isn't a reflection of your contribution. It's why the lab is structured the way it is. The vision has to stay intact while it propagates. That requires the structure, not just the philosophy.

If anything here doesn't match your understanding of your role, that conversation happens with the founder before work continues.

## What to Work On Right Now

**Collaborator agreement tracking system.** Every active collaborator across all four projects needs a signed agreement that specifies their tier, weight, compensation structure, IP transfer, and exit terms. Map who has one and who doesn't. Work with legal to get outstanding agreements drafted and executed.

**Quarterly review process.** Define what a quarterly project review actually looks like — what gets covered, what gets documented, what the output is. Then run the first one for each active project. This is the function that earns bonus points and the function that keeps the founder informed without requiring four separate conversations.

**Disbursement readiness tracking.** Build a simple picture of where each project is relative to its runway threshold. What is the threshold? What is the current runway? What is the trajectory? This should be something you can report in one conversation without having to go look things up.

**Onboarding process documentation.** The lab is adding collaborators. Each one goes through onboarding. Right now that process is informal. Make it a repeatable sequence — what they read, what they sign, what access they get, who confirms it's done.

**Project status baseline.** For each active project (IΔ, WAXFEED, GPS, AVDP), establish the current state: active collaborators and tiers, outstanding agreements, milestone status, runway status, next milestone. This is the starting point for everything else you do.

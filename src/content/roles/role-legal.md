## What You're Here For

The lab's financial and governance structure is deliberately unconventional. It works because it's been designed carefully, but that design is only as good as the legal instruments that implement it. Revenue participation agreements, IP transfer clauses, collaborator agreements, Reg D compliance, patent strategy — none of these hold up without someone who can draft them, review them, and tell the lab when it's about to do something it can't undo.

This is not a general counsel role in the traditional sense. The lab is not a corporation managing routine legal operations. It's an early-stage research institution that needs precise answers to specific questions, clean agreement templates, and a clear picture of where the legal exposure is. You're the person who provides that.

---

## What You Do

You answer the open questions in the legal brief. There are twelve. They cover securities structure, employment classification, IP transfer, broker-dealer obligations, loss disclosure language, patent filing strategy, and instrument licensing. These are not hypothetical questions. They are blockers on specific actions the lab needs to take. Work through them in order of operational urgency — the collaborator agreement template and the Reg D structure are most time-sensitive.

You draft the agreement templates. The lab needs: a collaborator agreement (covering tier, weight, IP transfer, vesting, and exit terms), a revenue participation agreement for $5,000+ financial believers (covering pro-rata share, year-1 window, no governance rights, break-even disclosure), and a scope-of-work template for IΔ institutional assessments. These are the three instruments that unlock the next phase of the lab's operations. Nothing meaningful can be signed until they exist.

You review structures before the lab commits to them. The financial model, the grant applications, the institutional assessment agreements — when the lab is about to sign or publish something with legal implications, you see it first. Not to slow things down but to make sure the lab isn't creating an obligation it doesn't understand.

You build the IP protection strategy. The lab's instruments are its core assets. Integrity Delta is defined and piloted. The others (working names: Proximity Index, Cognitive Gap Measure, Format Effectiveness Measure) are in development and their final names and definitions are not yet confirmed. The methodology needs protection before it's widely published. You advise on what can be patented, what's better protected as trade secret, what timing makes sense relative to publication.

You flag implications the lab hasn't seen. The founder and domain leads think about research and product. Legal implications of operational decisions are not always visible to them. When you see something that creates exposure, surface it before it becomes a problem.

---

## What Stays With the Founder

Which agreements to sign and on what terms is the founder's decision. You advise on the legal implications of terms. You don't negotiate on the lab's behalf without explicit authorization.

Financial structure decisions — whether to accept a particular investor, how to structure a specific agreement, whether to open a believer pool — belong to the founder. You tell the founder what the legal constraints are. The decision is his.

Research agenda and publication timing are not legal decisions. If a legal consideration touches those areas (a funder agreement that restricts publication, for example), you flag it as a legal problem. You don't weigh in on the research substance.

---

## How You Get Paid

You earn points through the core pool each quarter. Your base is 15 points per active month. You earn 10 bonus points for each agreement executed and 15 bonus points for each patent filed.

At each disbursement, your share of the core pool equals your points divided by total points earned across all core pool roles that quarter. Points reset quarterly.

This does not start until the lab hits its runway threshold. The full mechanics are in the fund model document, shared when you join.

---

## Ownership and Governance

Polarity Lab LLC is Theodore's. You are a collaborator, not an owner. You have no governance rights over the lab's direction, research agenda, or financial decisions.

Work product you produce for the lab — agreement templates, legal memoranda, patent applications — belongs to the lab.

If anything here doesn't match your understanding of your role, that conversation happens with the founder before work continues.

---

## What to Work On Right Now

**Collaborator agreement template.** Every person doing work for the lab needs something signed. This is the most operationally urgent document the lab needs. It must cover: tier and weight, IP transfer at project start, vesting schedule, performance review mechanism, exit terms before and after disbursements begin, and confirmation that the collaborator holds no governance rights.

**Revenue participation agreement template.** The $5,000+ financial believer tier cannot open until this exists. It must cover: pro-rata share calculation, year-1 window from first disbursement, no equity or governance rights, break-even disclosure, loss disclosure, and Reg D compliance language.

**Answer legal brief questions 1-4 first.** Gift tier securities status, Reg D 506(b) confirmation, the agreement template, and the vesting mechanism language. These four unblock the most near-term operational decisions.

**IΔ scope-of-work template.** The first proof contract cannot be signed without it. Covers: assessment scope, deliverables, timeline, pricing, IP ownership of findings, and confidentiality terms.

## What You're Here For

The lab's core principle is that the demonstration is the marketing. That principle requires someone to produce the demonstrations — to translate what the lab is doing into language that lands with the right people without dumbing it down, losing the precision, or turning a genuine research finding into a content asset.

That's what this role is. Not brand management. Not content volume. Not growth metrics. The lab owns a specific vocabulary — second threshold, polite malpractice, authentic taste recovery, proximity-based discovery — and the job is to make sure that vocabulary spreads attached to its meaning, not stripped of it.

Read the marketing strategy document before doing anything. The approach is specific and generic content marketing actively damages it.

---

## What You Do

You own the vocabulary across every public surface. The lab's concepts — second threshold, Integrity Delta, Polite Malpractice, the return — need to show up consistently in how the lab describes itself, how projects describe themselves, and how the founder is quoted or cited. Inconsistent vocabulary is how ideas get flattened. You're the one who keeps them sharp.

You build and manage the content that makes the lab findable. Not content for its own sake — content that demonstrates the work. A post about what the IΔ pilot found is not marketing copy, it's a finding described for a clinical AI audience. A piece about what WAXFEED's data shows is not a product announcement, it's evidence of the instrument working. The content is the demonstration. You produce it with that standard.

You own the founder's public presence. Op-eds, speaking invitations, podcast appearances, published commentary on AI and medicine or music and technology — these are the channels where the lab's methodology becomes associated with a name and a perspective. You identify the right opportunities, prepare the right material, and make sure the founder's voice in public sounds like the lab sounds.

You track what's landing. Which descriptions of the work are generating the right conversations. Which audiences are resonating. Which vocabulary is traveling. This is not vanity metrics — it's signal on whether the lab's framing is working or needs refinement.

You coordinate with sales on the gift tier pipeline. Marketing brings people to awareness. Sales converts that awareness into gift tier backing. The handoff between those two functions needs to be clean — the right people reaching out to the right person at the right time.

---

## What Stays With the Founder

The lab's intellectual positions are the founder's to set. You translate and distribute them. You don't originate them or adjust them for audience palatability. If a position needs softening to land with a particular audience, the answer is a different audience, not a softer position.

Research findings are published by the researchers. You don't describe findings publicly before the relevant domain lead and founder have approved the description. A single inaccurate public description of IΔ's pilot results is harder to walk back than a delayed post.

Institutional relationships belong to the Director of Strategy and Partnerships. Marketing builds awareness with broad audiences. Strategic relationships are Nathan's domain.

---

## How You Get Paid

Marketing sits at the intersection of the core pool and project-level work. Lab-wide marketing — founder presence, vocabulary, cross-project positioning — is compensated through the core pool points system. Project-specific marketing work — WAXFEED content, IΔ outreach materials, GPS audience building — is compensated as a contributing collaborator within that project's time collaborator pool.

At the start of each quarter, the split between your lab-level and project-level work is agreed and documented. The full mechanics for both are in the fund model document, shared when you join.

## Ownership and Governance

Polarity Lab LLC is Theodore's. You are a collaborator, not an owner. You have no governance rights over the lab's direction, research agenda, or messaging positions.

Content you produce for the lab belongs to the lab.

If anything here doesn't match your understanding of your role, that conversation happens with the founder before work continues.

## What to Work On Right Now

**Vocabulary document.** A single internal reference for how the lab's core concepts are described: second threshold, Polite Malpractice, the four instruments, the return, the algorithm, the counter-environment. Definitions, the words that belong and the ones that don't, the precision that needs to be maintained. This is the foundation for everything public-facing.

**IΔ audience map.** Clinical AI vendors, hospital CMOs, AI safety researchers, medical ethicists, FDA digital health staff — who are the specific people and publications where the IΔ methodology needs to land? Map them, find where they congregate, identify the right format for reaching them.

**Founder content calendar.** What are the one or two pieces the founder could publish in the next 90 days that would make the lab findable to the right people? Not a volume target. The one piece that demonstrates the finding most clearly for the most relevant audience.

**WAXFEED pre-launch audience.** Before the platform exists, there should be a community of people who understand what it's trying to do and are waiting for it. Who are those people? Where are they? What would bring them in?
